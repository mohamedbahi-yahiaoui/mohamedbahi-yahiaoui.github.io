My research asks a practical question: how can we make machine learning models useful in environments where errors are expensive, distribution shift is common, and domain constraints matter? Across imaging and multiphysics simulation, I have worked on models that are not only accurate, but also interpretable, monitorable, and computationally efficient.

My thesis addresses this through two complementary directions. First, I developed a runtime verification and monitoring framework for detecting out-of-model-scope inputs, culminating in the **Bounding Box Anomaly Score (BBAS)** approach for out-of-distribution detection. Second, I designed and assessed neural surrogate models for scientific codes used in multiphysics nuclear-fuel simulation, with a focus on operational reliability and stability over long trajectories.

## Where I Can Contribute Beyond My Thesis Domain

The specific application context of my recent work is nuclear simulation, but the core skills transfer well beyond that setting. The problems I work on are the same ones many teams face today: detecting distribution shift, quantifying uncertainty, making models reliable enough for deployment, accelerating expensive computations, and building ML pipelines that scientists and engineers can actually trust.

That makes my profile relevant not only for labs in scientific computing, but also for groups working on trustworthy AI, computer vision, inverse problems, simulation and digital twins, medical or industrial imaging, and research engineering for complex data-driven systems.

    <p>

      <strong>about 100x faster</strong> prediction than reference simulation workflows while supporting reliability monitoring and fallback strategies.
